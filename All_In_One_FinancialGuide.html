ESTIMATED COST OF ATTENDANCE

Includes the average costs for a student to attend during the academic year, and is used to determine aid packages only. It is not an official invoice or billing statement. More information about the Estimated Cost of Attendance may be found at your Financial Aid Office website.

Each portion of your budget is allocated as follows:

Tuition and Fees Includes systemwide and campus-based tuition and fees, as well as other fees associated with enrollment. Actual tuition, fees, and charges are subject to change by the Regents of the University of California and could be affected by State funding reductions. Accordingly, final approved levels (and thus a student’s final balance due) may differ from the amounts shown.

Books and Supplies Covers the purchase of books and supplies required for your courses.  

Room & Board Includes housing fees or rent, utilities, telephone, meals, and groceries. Graduate and Professional students only have one Room & Board budget available.  

AVAILABLE HOUSING TYPES FOR UNDERGRADUATES

  • On-Campus: Students must have a recognized contract through UC Student Housing and pay costs quarter-by-quarter via MyBill.
  • Off-Campus: Students who sign a lease through a leaser other than UC Student Housing and typically pay rent on a month-to-month basis.  This includes students living physically on campus that do not have a contract directly with UC Student Housing.
  • Commuter: Students living with a parent.

Personal Expenses Includes clothing, entertainment, and incidentals. For graduate and professional students, this category also includes medical/dental/optical.

Health Insurance This component will include the mandatory Student Health Insurance Plan (SHIP) fee when applicable. This fee can be waived if you can provide adequate proof of private insurance.

Transportation Covers expected transportation costs, which include gas, basic car maintenance and a round trip train or bus ticket home.  

Summer Includes fees and costs for attending summer. This budget component applies to undergraduates, and will only be posted if applicable.   

Other Costs Includes additional basic educational cost considerations not categorized in the main budget components. This may comprise of, but is not limited to, loan fees, the entering undergraduate document fee, or additional expenses approved through an appeal. This budget component will only be posted if applicable.

TOTAL ESTIMATED COST OF ATTENDANCE

The total estimated cost of attending UC_____ for the academic year. This is the sum of all the budget components. Estimated Cost of Attendance amounts are subject to change.

CONTRIBUTIONS

Includes the amount(s) student's and/or their families are required to contribute toward your educational expenses. This section is comprised of the following components:

Student Self-Help This is the amount that undergraduates may expect to pay towards educational expenses that are not covered by their financial aid. This expectation may be covered with loan, scholarships or earnings from employment.  Self-Help does not apply to graduate/professional students.

Expected Family Contribution (EFC) The EFC is calculated by the federal or state processor based on information submitted on the Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA) or California Dream Act Application. A variety of loan options may be available to help students and families meet their required contribution. Contact your Financial Aid Office for more information.

Additional Resources Additional financial resources, such as: fee waivers, outside scholarships, stipends, private loans, or a variety of other sources of funds.  

Total Contributions The sum of Self-Help, Expected Family Contribution (EFC) and Additional Resources figures.

FINANCIAL NEED

The student's total eligibility for need-based financial aid. This represents the Total Estimated Cost of Attendance less the Total Contributions. If a student's Total Contributions exceed the Total Estimated Cost of Attendance, they are not eligible for need-based financial aid. In that case, the Financial Aid Office can offer Direct Unsubsidized Loans, Direct PLUS (Parent Loans for Undergraduate Students), Direct PLUS Graduate Loans, or Private and/or Alternative Loans, which are not based on need.

ESTIMATED FINANCIAL AID

Includes an estimate of the financial aid award(s) that a student is currently eligible for, such as: grants, scholarships, fellowships, loans, etc. This may include both need- and merit-based awards. The amount and types of awards offered may change based on a student's additional resources,  enrollment changes, or eligibility changes as a result of the verification process. For an understanding of how your awards will be paid during the academic year, please see the Estimated Financial Aid by Term section at the bottom of the budget page.

REMAINING COST

Should your Estimated Financial Aid not fully meet your Financial Need, a variety of loan options are available to help students and families meet the amount shown. Students are advised to contact their Financial Aid Office for more information.

NOTE: Remaining Cost does not change if you accept less than the full amount of an award being offered.

LOANS

Fed Direct Unsubsidized Loan
Federal Direct Unsubsidized Loans are not based on financial need.  Interest accrues beginning the day the loan is disbursed until the loan is repaid in full.  Repayment of principal and interest begins six months after graduation or withdrawal from school.

Fed Direct Subsidized Loans

 Loans made to eligible undergraduate students who demonstrate financial need to help cover the costs of higher education at a college or career school.

The U.S. Department of Education pays the interest on a Direct Subsidized Loan

  • while you’re in school at least half-time,
  • for the first six months after you leave school (referred to as a grace period*), and
  • during a period of deferment (a postponement of loan payments).

APPLYING

To apply for a Direct Loan, you must first complete and submit the Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA®). Your school will use the information from your FAFSA to determine how much student aid you are eligible to receive. Direct Loans are generally included as part of your financial aid package.

TIME PERIOD LIMITS(Measured in academic years)

This time limit does not apply to Direct Unsubsidized Loans or Direct PLUS Loans. If this limit applies to you, you may not receive Direct Subsidized Loans for more than 150 percent of the published length of your program. This is called your “maximum eligibility period.” Your maximum eligibility period is generally based on the published length of your current program. You can usually find the published length of any program of study in your school’s catalog.

For example, if you are enrolled in a four-year bachelor’s degree program, the maximum period for which you can receive Direct Subsidized Loans is six years (150 percent of 4 years = 6 years). If you are enrolled in a two-year associate degree program, the maximum period for which you can receive Direct Subsidized Loans is three years (150 percent of 2 years = 3 years).

Direct PLUS (Parent Loans for Undergraduate Students)

Loans made to graduate or professional students and parents of dependent undergraduate students to help pay for education expenses not covered by other financial aid.

Am I eligible for a Direct PLUS Loan?

To receive a Direct PLUS Loan, you must

  • be a graduate or professional student enrolled at least half-time at an eligible school in a program leading to a graduate or professional degree or certificate, or be the parent (biological, adoptive, or in some cases, stepparent) of a dependent undergraduate student enrolled at least half-time at an eligible school;
  • not have an adverse credit history; and
  • meet the general eligibility requirements for federal student aid. If you are borrowing on behalf of your child, your child must also meet these requirements.

APPLYING

To receive a Direct PLUS Loan, you (or your child, if you are a parent PLUS borrower) must complete the FAFSA®. The school's financial aid office will provide instructions about their process for requesting a Direct PLUS Loan. Many schools require that you request a Direct PLUS Loan at StudentLoans.gov. The request form has a list of schools that participate in the Direct PLUS Loan Program; if you select your (or your child’s) school from the list, the site will notify you if your school does not use the StudentLoans.gov process. If that is the case, check with the school to find out how to request a PLUS loan.  

Private and/or Alternative Loans

Offered by external companies that accrue a certain degree of interest over time. Frequently the rates and prices are higher than federally offered and allocated funding.

Federal Perkins Loan Program, often called Perkins Loans, are low-interest federal student loans for undergraduate and graduate students with exceptional financial need.

Here’s a quick overview of Federal Perkins Loans:

  • Available to undergraduate, graduate, and professional students with exceptional financial need.
  • Interest rate for this loan is 5%.
  • Not all schools participate in the Federal Perkins Loan Program. You should check with your school's financial aid office to see if your school participates.
  • Your school is the lender; you will make your payments to the school that made your loan or your school’s loan servicer.
  • Funds depend on your financial need and the availability of funds at your college.

WORK STUDY PROGRAM

What is it?

Federal work study is a need-based form of Financial Aid that you earn through on or off-campus employment and receive in the form of a paycheck. The intent of the Work-Study Program is to encourage part-time employment and reduce the amount of loan debt incurred while attending college. Work-Study does so by paying a portion of your salary thus making it more advantageous for an employer to hire you. Lastly, any earnings made under work-study do not count against your aid eligibility when filling out the next year’s FAFSA or California Dream Act application.

What types of jobs are available or recommended?

Differs by campus. Davis recommends community service positions in health care, child care, literacy training, education, mentoring, welfare, social services, transportation, housing and neighborhood improvement, public safety, recreation, community improvement, and

support services for students with disabilities. Other campuses encourage on campus and/or off campus part time work.

Eligibility Requirements as of 2017

Students with Expected Family Contributions (EFC) of less than or equal to $15,000 and eligibility greater than or equal to $1,000 will be identified on their award notices as Work-Study eligible. Work-Study funds will be awarded on a first-come, first-served basis. Each campus designates the eligibility requirements differently as well depending on school funding- some may be lower in EFC requisites.

AWARD STATUS

Declined:  The award is declined. If you wish to reconsider and accept the award, you will need to contact your Financial Aid Office. Reinstatement of the award depends on funds available at the time of your request.
Estimated:  The award amount shown is an estimate. The award is neither finalized nor funded, but your budget has been temporarily offset to anticipate the amount shown. Additional requirements may need to be completed before the award can be offered.
Offered:  Financial aid is offered after we  establish your eligibility and determined your financial need according to the submitted financial aid application. To receive the aid offered, you must accept the amount and submit any additional documentation if requested.
Accepted:  This award is accepted. All aid is subject to reduction, cancellation, or denial based on changes to your eligibility.

HIGHLY SIGNIFICANT - SCHOLARSHIPS

When can you start applying?

Any year!! As soon as you are a freshman in high school or even younger! There are scholarships even for middle school students so start researching early!

Do you recommend larger or smaller scholarships?

Overall the chances for getting a smaller scholarship is much higher due to the smaller degree of competition. However, that does not mean your list of achievements and effort loses out to any other. I say go for both! It is the number of scholarships you apply to and the perseverance that wins out in overall financial aid! The more you apply, the higher your opportunities to win a scholarship. Make sure you fulfill all listed prerequisites before applying.

What is Regents?

The Regents Scholarship, based on academic excellence, is the most prestigious scholarship awarded to UC undergraduate students. Regents Scholars receive an honorarium of up to $8,000, paid $2,000 in each of their 4 or 2 undergraduate years (for freshmen or transfer students), plus special privileges as outlined on UC website. Eligibility also requires filling out FAFSA.

What about Chancellors?

UC San Diego awards Chancellor's Scholarships to entering freshmen on the basis of outstanding academic achievement. Chancellor's Scholars receive an honorarium of up to $5,000 per year in each of their 4 undergraduate years (up to $20,000 total), plus special privileges as outlined below.
